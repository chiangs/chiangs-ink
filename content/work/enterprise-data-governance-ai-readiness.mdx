## The Problem

Large enterprises don't fail at data because they
lack data. They fail because the data they have
is ungoverned, inconsistently defined, and
impossible to trust at scale.

For one of the world's largest oil and gas companies,
this wasn't a technical problem — it was an
organisational one. A federated governance model
meant data ownership, definitions, and quality
standards varied across business units and domains.
The ambition to leverage AI for automated and
supported master data management was real, but
the foundation wasn't there yet. Before any
intelligent system could reason over the data,
the data itself had to be understood, mapped,
and governed.

<Challenge>
In a federated enterprise of this scale and
complexity, how do you create a coherent data
governance strategy — and a credible path to
AI-automated master data management — without
imposing a centralised model the organisation
won't adopt?
</Challenge>

## The Process

As part of a service design team, I contributed
as Design Technologist — bringing the technical
lens to what was fundamentally a strategic and
organisational engagement.

**Data Landscape Mapping**
The first task was understanding what existed
and where. We mapped the data landscape across
the organisation — identifying domains, ownership
structures, upstream sources, and downstream
dependencies. In a federated model of this
complexity, this is not a trivial exercise.
Data means different things to different parts
of the business, and those differences have
real consequences when you're trying to build
anything on top of them.

**Governance Assessment**
We assessed the current state of data governance
against the organisation's ambitions — identifying
where the gaps were between how data was being
managed today and what would be required to
support reliable, trustworthy data products
at enterprise scale. Although the primary focus
was the human experience of master data management,
our research and analysis surfaced findings that
extended well beyond the data team — revealing
operational insights and implications relevant
to frontline teams and senior stakeholders
that hadn't been visible before the engagement.

**Tooling Strategy**
A critical deliverable was the assessment and
prescription of tools to improve data management
and data product governance. Not just what tools
exist in the market, but which tools fit this
organisation's federated model, existing
infrastructure, and realistic adoption capacity.

<PullQuote>
The goal wasn't to centralise control.
It was to create the conditions where
federated ownership and enterprise-wide
data quality could coexist.
</PullQuote>

## The Outcome

The engagement delivered a data governance
strategy and tooling assessment that gave the
organisation a clear, actionable path forward —
from current state to a governed data foundation
capable of supporting AI-automated and
AI-assisted master data management.

For an organisation of this scale operating
a federated model, the value isn't in a single
delivered system. It's in the strategic clarity
that aligns multiple business units around a
shared direction — and the technical credibility
to know that direction is buildable.

What the engagement also revealed was the
value of approaching a data problem through
a human lens. By centring the research on
how people actually interact with master data
— not just how it flows through systems —
we uncovered operational friction and
stakeholder pain points that a purely
technical assessment would have missed.
The findings were relevant not just to
the data governance team, but to the
operational teams living with the
consequences of ungoverned data every day.

The data foundation work positions the organisation
to move from manual, inconsistent master data
management toward intelligent automation —
without the governance debt that makes most
enterprise AI initiatives fail before they start.
